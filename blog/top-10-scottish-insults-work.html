Top 10 Scottish Insults You Can Actually Say at Work

May 2025

Let's be honest — work can test even the saintliest of patience. Whether it's meetings that should've been emails or coworkers who couldn't organise a paper jam in a printer factory, we all have our moments.

But before you lob a full-strength F-bomb and end up explaining yourself to HR, why not spice things up with a bit of Scottish flavor? Here are 10 workplace-safe Scottish insults that'll let you blow off steam without losing your job.

1. "You're a right numpty."

Translation: "You've done something unbelievably daft."

Perfect for: That teammate who just replied-all to the company-wide email chain.

It's light, it's funny, and let's face it — they probably are.

2. "Away and bile yer heid."

Literal translation: "Go boil your head."

Practical meaning: "Take a moment. Rethink your life choices."

Use this one when someone pitches yet another meeting about meetings.

3. "Ye're a wee glaikit."

Glaikit = vacant, dazed, one sandwich short of a picnic.

Use sparingly and only with trusted coworkers who'll laugh — not weep — when you say it.

4. "That's pure dead brilliant."

Said sarcastically, this is gold.

Perfect for: Reviewing a truly tragic slide deck.

Bonus: Sounds like a compliment, feels like a dagger.

5. "Ye're a right bampot."

Bampot = lovable lunatic.

For the coworker who's cracked but harmless, this is affectionate shade at its finest.

6. "Away and don't talk mince."

Mince = nonsense, gibberish, the verbal equivalent of spreadsheet soup.

Perfect when someone's theory is built entirely on vibes and zero facts.

7. "Ye're a right jobby."

Jobby = turd.

Yes, we're technically calling them poop — but in the most whimsical, PG-rated way possible.

8. "That's a load of keech."

Keech = rubbish, crap, total nonsense.

Use this when someone presents an idea so off-base it deserves a polite chuckle and a firm "nope."

9. "Ye're a walloper."

Walloper = a person who's being loud, annoying, or full of themselves.

Great for: That one guy who always interrupts and thinks he's the smartest person in the room. (He's not.)

10. "Away and chase yersel."

This one's pure magic.

Meaning: "Get lost," but in the most delightfully Scottish way.

Use it when you need space — like when someone schedules a 4:45 p.m. Friday meeting.

A Wee Word of Warning

Yes, these are workplace-friendly(ish), but delivery is everything. Use them with a smile, a wink, and maybe a biscuit in hand. And if your office has the sense of humor of a damp scone, maybe keep it to "numpty" and save "walloper" for the pub.
